From one honest proof to a research paper

Lecture 4 got us a first kernel-checked proof: Nat, induction, a starter kit of tactics. Enough to reprove undergraduate lemmas one keystroke at a time — not enough to do mathematics at scale.

Two things stand in the way:

  • Generality — state a * b = b * a once, at the right level of abstraction, and have it fire on $\N$, $\Z$, polynomials, matrices.
  • Automation & search — no human recalls a library this size. The skill is finding lemmas and letting decision procedures discharge the routine algebra.
Name the gap honestly: what stands between "I can prove zero plus n equals n" and "I can formalize a research paper"? Two things. Generality — nobody wants to reprove commutativity for the naturals, then the integers, then polynomials, then matrices; you want to state it once, at the right abstraction, and have it fire everywhere. Dependent types: the one primitive

What separates Lean from a simply-typed language is the dependent function type (Π-type): given $\alpha$ and a family $\beta : \alpha \to \mathrm{Sort}\,u$,

$$\prod_{x:\alpha} \beta\,x \quad\text{written}\quad (x : \alpha) \to \beta\,x \quad\text{or}\quad \forall\,x,\ \beta\,x .$$

The ordinary arrow $\alpha \to \beta$ is the degenerate case where $\beta$ ignores $x$. So $\forall$ and $\to$ are the same connective: a proof of ∀ n, P n is a function sending each n to a proof of P n. Types depend on values — Vector α n carries its length in its type.

Slow down here — this is the one primitive that separates Lean from a simply-typed language. The ordinary arrow is just the degenerate Π-type where the family ignores its argument, which is why ∀ and → are the same connective. Say it plainly and twice: a proof of "for all n, P n" is a function that sends each n to a proof of P n. And types depend on values — Vector α n carries its length in its type, a matrix carries its shape.

Prop vs Type — and why it matters

Everything lives in the universe hierarchy:

$$\mathrm{Prop} = \mathrm{Sort}\,0, \qquad \mathrm{Type}\,u = \mathrm{Sort}\,(u+1).$$

  • Proof irrelevance. For p : Prop and proofs h₁ h₂ : p, they are definitionally equal: $h_1 \equiv h_2$. A proposition carries no data beyond being inhabited.
  • Large-elimination restriction. You cannot pattern-match a proof of ∃ n, P n to extract the witness as data — that would leak a non-computational choice through a proof-irrelevant door.

De Bruijn criterion: only the small kernel is trusted; #print axioms thm audits which axioms a proof really used.

ch type '((P -> Q) -> P) -> P'

Peirce's law comes back UNINHABITED — no λ-term proves it in intuitionistic STLC. That is exactly why classical principles enter Lean as axioms (Classical.choice), and why #print axioms has something to report.

Insist that this split is not cosmetic. Proof irrelevance means a proposition carries no data beyond being inhabited — and that is exactly why you cannot pattern-match an existence proof to pull the witness out: two definitionally-equal proofs could then yield different data, a contradiction. Then run the lab command: Peirce's law comes back uninhabited, no λ-term proves it, and that is precisely why classical principles must enter Lean as axioms and why #print axioms has anything to report. In Prop, inhabited is all there is.

Structures & typeclasses: the algebraic hierarchy

A structure bundles data with the proofs of its axioms. A class is a structure whose instances Lean finds automatically — the mechanism behind +, 0, ≤ meaning the right thing on every type at once.

class Group (G : Type*) extends DivInvMonoid G where
  inv_mul_cancel : ∀ a : G, a⁻¹ * a = 1

State a theorem under [CommMonoid M] and it fires everywhere: $\texttt{mul\_comm} : a * b = b * a$ — proved once, inherited everywhere. The price is the diamond problem: two inheritance paths to a shared ancestor must give definitionally-equal instances.

A structure is a one-constructor inductive type bundling data with the proofs of its axioms; a class is a structure whose instances Lean finds automatically — and that is the entire mechanism behind plus, zero, and less-or-equal meaning the right thing on every type at once. Show the real Mathlib Group class: a group is literally a monoid-with-inverses plus one axiom. The payoff to hammer: mul_comm is proved once under CommMonoid and inherited by naturals, integers, polynomials, and matrices alike. Then name the price — diamonds: two inheritance paths to a shared ancestor must give definitionally-equal instances, or Lean sees two different plus signs on the same type and resolution breaks.

Interface over definition

class MyAddMonoid (α : Type*) where
  zero    : α
  add     : α → α → α
  add_zero : ∀ a, add a zero = a
  zero_add : ∀ a, add zero a = a

instance : MyAddMonoid Nat where
  zero := 0 ; add := Nat.add
  add_zero := Nat.add_zero ; zero_add := Nat.zero_add

open MyAddMonoid in
theorem add_zero_zero {α : Type*} [MyAddMonoid α] (a : α) :
    add (add a zero) zero = a := by
  rw [add_zero, add_zero]

The proof never mentions Nat — it reasons from the interface, so it holds for every future instance for free.

Walk the code top to bottom, then ask the room: where does the final proof mention Nat? It never does. It reasons purely from add_zero in the class interface, so the theorem automatically holds for every instance anyone ever registers. This is "interface over definition" — the discipline that lets Mathlib prove each lemma once and reuse it across the entire algebraic hierarchy.

How to search Mathlib

Decode names left-to-right: Finset.sum_range_succ = a sum over range (n+1), split at the last term.

Search in four modalities:

  • In-editor: exact?, apply?, rw?, simp? (pins a robust simp only [...]).
  • Loogle — syntactic: search by shape, e.g. "(_ * _) ^ _". Complete and predictable.
  • LeanSearch — neural/semantic: query in natural language when you don't know the shape.
  • Moogle — embedding-based, similar in spirit.

Reflex: exact? first, Loogle when you can describe the shape, LeanSearch when you can only describe the idea.

Names are not arbitrary: they read left-to-right as the shape of the conclusion, namespaced by the head symbol, so you can reconstruct a statement from its name before ever searching — practice on Finset.sum_range_succ. Then hand them the reflex to memorize: exact? first, Loogle when you can describe the shape, LeanSearch when you can only describe the idea. Say the trade-off out loud: formal engines are complete and literal, neural engines forgive vague intent. And often — as with the √2 lemma coming up — the search simply is the proof.

The tactic zoo — match the goal's shape

  • ring — commutative-(semi)ring identities; ignores hypotheses
  • linear_combination e — equality as a certificate of hypotheses
  • linarith / nlinarith — linear / nonlinear ordered arithmetic
  • omega — linear integer/nat arithmetic, complete
  • norm_num — closed numeric goals
  • field_simp — clear denominators, hand off to ring
  • positivity — prove $0 < e$, $0 \le e$, $e \neq 0$
  • decide — a Decidable prop, by kernel computation

New (Lean 4.22): grind, SMT-style, ships cutsat (supersedes omega) and a Gröbner solver (grobner). polyrith is retired — its Sage server was shut down.

The skill here is matching each goal to the decision procedure that owns its shape, so students stop hand-rolling algebra — do not try to cover every row, just the reading of the table. Warn about the two newcomer traps explicitly: ring proves identities in the free commutative ring and therefore ignores your hypotheses — a hypothesis-driven equality needs linear_combination or nlinarith — and linarith is strictly linear, so nonlinear goals need nlinarith fed the right square hints. Close with the 2025–26 news: grind is the new SMT-style workhorse, and polyrith is retired because its external Sage server was shut down.

Inequalities in one line: hand nlinarith the square

Cauchy–Schwarz, because the gap is a perfect square $(ay-bx)^2 \ge 0$:

theorem cauchy_schwarz (a b x y : ℝ) :
    (a*x + b*y)^2 ≤ (a^2 + b^2) * (x^2 + y^2) := by
  nlinarith [sq_nonneg (a*y - b*x)]

theorem amgm (a b : ℝ) (ha : 0 ≤ a) (hb : 0 ≤ b) :
    2 * Real.sqrt (a*b) ≤ a + b := by
  nlinarith [sq_nonneg (a - b), Real.sq_sqrt (mul_nonneg ha hb),
             Real.sqrt_nonneg (a*b)]

Contrast a purely finite goal, closed by evaluation — Wilson at $p=7$: example : Nat.factorial 6 % 7 = 6 := by decide.

The lesson of this slide: automation does the algebra, but the human supplies the insight. Cauchy–Schwarz is one nlinarith call because the gap between the two sides is exactly the perfect square (ay − bx)² — your entire contribution is handing over that hint. AM–GM is the same trick plus the defining fact that the square of √(ab) is ab. Then contrast the Wilson example: a purely finite goal needs no cleverness at all — decide closes it by kernel computation.

calc — proofs that read like the blackboard

Given $a^2 = b^2 + 4$ and $a + b = 2$, show $a - b = 2$. The prose survives intact:

example (a b : ℝ) (h1 : a^2 = b^2 + 4) (h2 : a + b = 2) : a - b = 2 := by
  have hprod : (a - b) * (a + b) = 4 := by
    calc (a - b) * (a + b) = a^2 - b^2 := by ring
      _ = 4 := by rw [h1]; ring
  rw [h2] at hprod          -- hprod : (a - b) * 2 = 4
  linarith

Through the Trans typeclass a single calc may mix =, ≤, <, ∣, ⊆. This legibility is what convinces mathematicians the formal text is honest.

Read the calc block aloud to the room — it is the blackboard line verbatim: (a−b)(a+b) equals a² − b² equals 4. Point out Heather Macbeth's pattern, the idiom to actually learn: alternate have for auxiliary facts with calc for the computation, and close the linear tail with linarith. Mention that through the Trans typeclass one calc chain can mix equalities, inequalities, divisibility, and inclusion. And make the meta-point: this legibility is what convinces working mathematicians that the formal text is honest mathematics, not code.

Recursion: structural vs. well-founded

Structural

Every recursive call on a strict subterm. No termination proof; compiles to the recursor, emits equation lemmas. Mirror: the induction tactic.

Well-founded

Decreasing argument not a literal subterm (e.g. gcd on y % (x+1)). Supply termination_by + decreasing_by.

def mygcd : Nat → Nat → Nat
  | 0,     y => y
  | x + 1, y => mygcd (y % (x + 1)) (x + 1)
termination_by a _ => a
decreasing_by exact Nat.mod_lt _ (Nat.succ_pos x)

partial def escapes the kernel and produces no equation lemmas — you can prove nothing about it.

Which of the two you must use is dictated by the recursive call, not by taste. Structural recursion — every call on a strict subterm — needs no termination proof, compiles to the recursor, and hands you equation lemmas for free; its proof-side mirror is the induction tactic. Euclid's gcd is the classic non-subterm case: it recurses on y mod (x+1), so you supply termination_by and discharge the strict decrease with Nat.mod_lt. Land the warning hard: partial def escapes the kernel and emits no equation lemmas, so you can prove literally nothing about it.

One theorem end-to-end: $\sqrt 2$ is irrational

Standing on the library, it is one line:

theorem sqrt2_irrational : Irrational (Real.sqrt 2) := by
  exact_mod_cast Nat.Prime.irrational_sqrt (p := 2) Nat.prime_two

The instructive proof reconstructs the classical argument you now own:

$$2 q^2 = p^2 \Rightarrow 2 \mid p^2 \Rightarrow 2 \mid p \Rightarrow 4 \mid p^2 = 2q^2 \Rightarrow 2 \mid q,$$

contradicting $\gcd(p,q)=1$. The lesson: stand on Mathlib when the lemma exists, build it yourself when you need to see the machinery.

Show the two proofs of the same theorem side by side — that contrast is the whole slide. Standing on the library it is one line: Nat.Prime.irrational_sqrt at p equals 2, with exact_mod_cast bridging the coercion from the natural 2 to the real literal. The instructive version reconstructs the classical parity descent, and every step of it — by_contra, omega, the divisibility lemmas — is a tactic the students now own. Under the hood: the elaboration pipeline

Lean 4 is written in Lean 4, and your code can run at every stage:

parse ▶ macro-expand ▶ elaborate ▶ kernel type-check

macro "split_and" : tactic => `(tactic| refine ⟨?_, ?_⟩)

example (p q : Prop) (hp : p) (hq : q) : p ∧ q := by
  split_and
  · exact hp
  · exact hq

Punchline: term mode and tactic mode, simp and grind, your macro and Mathlib's — all produce the same object, an Expr, and only the kernel confers theoremhood.

prove (P -> Q) -> P -> Q

Tactic it in the browser — intro f · intro p · apply f · exact p · qed — and qed extracts the λ-term with its principal type: the tactic script and the term are the same object. Then lean term_proofs shows the identical two-modes-one-Expr fact in Lean.
