What, precisely, is a proof?

A first logic course decides formulas with truth tables: a tautology is true under every valuation.

A proof assistant cannot work this way. Its kernel does not evaluate a table — it checks a derivation, a syntactic object built from a fixed stock of inference rules.

Before Lean can help us with real mathematics, we must answer the question that opens this lecture.

Ask the room first: what did your first logic course say a proof was? Most will answer truth tables — a tautology is true under every valuation, and that is that. The point to land is that a kernel cannot evaluate a table; it checks a derivation, a syntactic object built from a fixed stock of inference rules. So the deceptively simple question "what, precisely, is a proof?" is the real content of this lecture.

Proofs = programs

The answer that makes machine-checked mathematics possible: a proof is a construction, and a construction is a typed program. This is the Curry–Howard correspondence.

  • every connective becomes a type former,
  • every inference rule becomes a term former,
  • every Lean tactic becomes a recipe that fills a hole in a proof term.

The same moves you learn here on toy propositions build the kernel-checked theorems of the Lecture 6 formalization.

Natural deduction — implication

Each connective gets introduction rules (how to prove it) and elimination rules (how to use it). Implication is subtle: introduction discharges a hypothesis $[A]$.

$$ \frac{\begin{array}{c}[A]\\ \vdots\\ B\end{array}}{A\to B}\;{\to}I \qquad\qquad \frac{A\to B \qquad A}{B}\;{\to}E $$

${\to}E$ is modus ponens. A derivation is a finite tree; the formula at the root is the conclusion.

Gentzen's design, from 1934: each connective gets introduction rules — how to prove it — and elimination rules — how to use it. Spend your time on discharge: the bracketed [A] is an assumption that gets "used up", closed off, by the rule below it, and this is the one genuinely subtle mechanism in the whole calculus. Ask the room to hold modus ponens in mind — within twenty minutes it will literally be function application.

Conjunction, disjunction, falsity

$$ \frac{A \quad B}{A\land B}\;{\land}I \qquad \frac{A\land B}{A}\;{\land}E_1 \qquad \frac{A\land B}{B}\;{\land}E_2 $$

$$ \frac{A}{A\lor B}\;{\lor}I_1 \qquad \frac{B}{A\lor B}\;{\lor}I_2 \qquad \frac{A\lor B \quad [A]\Rightarrow C \quad [B]\Rightarrow C}{C}\;{\lor}E $$

Falsity has an elimination but no introduction — that absence is the whole point:

$$ \frac{\bot}{C}\;{\bot}E \quad(\text{ex falso quodlibet}). $$

${\lor}E$ is case analysis. Negation is derived: $\neg A := A\to\bot$.

Conjunction is routine; give disjunction elimination a full beat — it is case analysis, you must handle both possibilities, and each case may discharge its own assumption. Then point hard at falsity: it has an elimination but no introduction, and that absence is the whole point — it is what consistency means. If there is time, mention harmony: an introduction immediately followed by its matching elimination is a detour, Prawitz showed removing detours normalizes the derivation, and under Curry–Howard detour removal is exactly β-reduction — simplifying a proof is running a program.

BHK — what a proof is

Read each connective as a recipe for constructions, not as a truth value.

  • a proof of $A\land B$ is a pair $\langle a,b\rangle$;
  • a proof of $A\lor B$ is a tagged proof $(i,p)$ — you must know which side holds;
  • a proof of $A\to B$ is a function turning proofs of $A$ into proofs of $B$;
  • there is no proof of $\bot$;
  • a proof of $\neg A = A\to\bot$ is a map you apply, never something you evaluate.

The tag clause is why there is no construction witnessing $A\lor\neg A$ — you would have to decide $A$.

This is the conceptual pivot of the lecture: "proof" becomes a first-class object, which is exactly why it can later be a Lean term. Two clauses do all the real work. The disjunction tag means asserting A or B requires knowing which side holds — so ask the room: why is there no construction witnessing A or not-A? And warn about the negation clause explicitly: not-A is a map you apply to a proof of A, never a truth value you evaluate — that misreading is the most common stumble of the whole week.

Provability becomes inhabitation: $A$ is provable iff the type $A$ has a closed term.

Read a few rows aloud: implication is a function type, conjunction a pair with two projections, disjunction a sum used by case analysis. The bottom line is the theorem of the lecture — provability becomes inhabitation. In Lean this is completely literal: And is a structure, Or is an inductive type with two constructors, and False is the inductive type with no constructors at all. Howard and Prawitz make it precise: normal terms correspond to normal derivations, the calculus is strongly normalizing, so no closed term of type ⊥ exists — that is consistency.

Worked example — $A\land B\to B\land A$

The proof term is $\lambda h.\,\langle h.2,\,h.1\rangle$. Term mode and tactic mode build the same kernel object:

-- term mode: the proof IS the program
theorem and_comm' {P Q : Prop} : P ∧ Q → Q ∧ P :=
  fun h => ⟨h.2, h.1⟩

-- tactic mode: a recipe that fills the same hole
theorem and_comm'' {P Q : Prop} : P ∧ Q → Q ∧ P := by
  intro h            -- goal Q ∧ P, with h : P ∧ Q   (→I / λ)
  obtain ⟨p, q⟩ := h  -- destructure the pair         (∧E)
  exact ⟨q, p⟩        -- build the swapped pair        (∧I)
Walk the term first: h.2 and h.1 are the two projections, the angle brackets repackage them, and the lambda is the discharge of the hypothesis. Then run the tactic script line by line and insist on the punchline: term mode and tactic mode build the same kernel object — these are not two different logics, just two ways of writing one program. Mention that the disjunction analogue is the same shape with rcases splitting into two goals, one per injection.

Negation, constructively

Three theorems people wrongly assume need classical logic. All are pure constructions ($\neg\neg A = (A\to\bot)\to\bot$):

$$ A\to\neg\neg A,\qquad \neg(A\land\neg A),\qquad \neg\neg\neg A\to\neg A. $$

theorem dni       {P : Prop} : P → ¬¬P        := fun p k => k p
theorem noncontra {P : Prop} : ¬(P ∧ ¬P)      := fun h => h.2 h.1
theorem tne       {P : Prop} : ¬¬¬P → ¬P      := fun h p => h (fun k => k p)

The converse $\neg\neg A\to A$ is not available here — it is the classical boundary in disguise.

ch term \p k. k p

Algorithm W infers the principal type α → (α → β) → β — read β as $\bot$ and this is exactly $A\to\neg\neg A$: the program came first, the proposition is its type.

Have them trace dni by hand: given p, we must produce something of type (P → False) → False, so take the continuation k and apply it to p — negation is something you apply, remember. All three of these are pure constructions; poll the room on which ones they assumed needed classical logic. Then run the lab line: Algorithm W on the untyped term recovers the theorem as its principal type — the Curry-style direction, where the program comes first and the proposition is read off as its most general type. The converse, double-negation elimination, is exactly what is not available — hold that thought for two slides.

De Morgan — and its classical cousin

One equivalence is fully constructive; one implication is genuinely classical:

$$ \neg(A\lor B)\Leftrightarrow(\neg A\land\neg B)\ \text{(intuitionistic)},\qquad \neg(A\land B)\to(\neg A\lor\neg B)\ \text{(classical)}. $$

theorem deMorgan_or {P Q : Prop} : ¬(P ∨ Q) ↔ (¬P ∧ ¬Q) :=
  ⟨fun h => ⟨fun p => h (Or.inl p), fun q => h (Or.inr q)⟩,
   fun ⟨np, nq⟩ h => h.elim np nq⟩

The missing direction would force you to pick a tag — decide which conjunct fails. BHK forbids that.

Trace the proof term: the forward map builds each conjunct by feeding an injection into h, and the backward map takes the pair and case-splits on the alleged disjunction — only constructive moves throughout. Then dwell on the missing implication: to output not-A or not-B you would have to pick a tag, decide which conjunct fails, and BHK forbids conjuring that information out of thin air. This is the cleanest concrete example of a classically valid formula with no constructive content — it needs excluded middle.

The classical boundary

Equivalent over intuitionistic logic — adding any one recovers full classical logic:

$$ A\lor\neg A,\qquad \neg\neg A\to A,\qquad ((A\to B)\to A)\to A. $$

theorem peirce {P Q : Prop} : ((P → Q) → P) → P := by
  intro h
  by_contra hnp                     -- hnp : ¬P, goal : False
  exact hnp (h (fun p => absurd p hnp))

Every step but by_contra is constructive — the classical content is isolated in exactly one move. Glivenko: $\vdash_c A$ iff $\vdash_i \neg\neg A$.

ch type ((P -> Q) -> P) -> P

The lab's inhabitation search answers not inhabited in intuitionistic STLC — a machine check that no constructive witness exists. Compare ch type (P -> Q -> R) -> (P -> Q) -> P -> R, which finds one instantly.

State the equivalence first: adding any one of these three to intuitionistic logic recovers all of classical logic. Then trace the Peirce proof slowly — from hnp we can prove anything, so we manufacture the P → Q, feed it to h to get P, and feed that back to hnp for False — and emphasize that the classical content is isolated in exactly one move. The lab line is the machine verdict: inhabitation search confirms no constructive witness exists. Close with Glivenko — everything classical is a double negation away — and the pedagogical cost: a by_contra existence proof shows a counterexample cannot exist without ever handing you the object.

  • Every tactic builds a piece of the term; only by_contra crosses into classical logic.
