Why this matters

In about two years, machines went from solving essentially nothing at the IMO to olympiad gold.

  • Curry–Howard, live in 2026: proofs are objects a machine can check.
  • A proof assistant gives an absolute soundness guarantee relative to a tiny kernel.
  • A chatbot's natural-language "proof" gives none.

Between those poles sits autoformalization — and that is exactly where a green checkmark can still certify the wrong theorem.

Open by asking the room what changed in roughly two years — machines went from essentially nothing at the IMO to olympiad gold, and that single fact reframes the whole course. The Curry–Howard slogan from Lecture 2, that proofs are objects a machine can check, is now a live 2026 research programme. But stress the asymmetry: a proof assistant gives an absolute soundness guarantee relative to a tiny trusted kernel, while a chatbot's prose "proof" gives none. The transferable skill of this lecture is learning to see the gap in between — where a machine-checked green checkmark can still certify the wrong theorem.

Two problems, one kernel

Statement autoformalization

"$\sqrt 2$ is irrational" $\rightsquigarrow$ a precise Lean theorem ….

Hard part: faithfulness. No proof obligation discharged.

Proof autoformalization

Given a fixed statement, find a proof the kernel accepts.

Hard part: search. Every candidate is cheaply checkable.

The engine of the field: proofs are hard to find but easy to check — the model proposes, the kernel disposes.

Have students say the two halves back to you before moving on: statement autoformalization is judgement-laden — the formal statement must mean what the mathematician meant, and no proof obligation is discharged — while proof autoformalization is search-heavy, because the space of tactic scripts is vast but every candidate is cheaply checkable. That asymmetry, hard to find but easy to check, is the engine of the entire field. Mention the inverse map too: informalization, Lean back to prose, is used both for readability and as a round-trip faithfulness check.

The faithfulness gap: a compiling lie

The kernel certifies $\text{proof} \vdash \text{statement}$, never $\text{statement} \models \text{intent}$.

-- claim: "every prime > 2 is odd"
-- faithful:  ∀ p : ℕ, p.Prime → 2 < p → Odd p
-- but this also type-checks and is trivially provable:
theorem primes_are_odd (p : ℕ) (hp : p.Prime) (h : p = 4) : Odd p := by
  subst h; exact absurd hp (by decide)

p = 4 with p.Prime is never satisfiable — vacuously true. A statement can be vacuous, over- or under-constrained, and still type-check.

This slide is worth ten minutes of any lecture — do not rush it. Walk the code slowly: the hypothesis p = 4 together with p.Prime is never satisfiable, so the theorem is vacuously true and means nothing, yet the kernel is perfectly happy, because it checks the proof against this statement, not against the mathematician's sentence. Then scale the worry up: AlphaProof auto-formalized on the order of a million competition problems and Goedel-Prover 1.64 million NuminaMath statements, so an unfaithful-but-well-typed statement is the pipeline's core, kernel-invisible failure mode. That is exactly why ProofNet grades faithfulness, not just type-correctness.

The neural–symbolic loop

Model proposes a tactic $\to$ assistant returns the new goal or an error $\to$ search chooses what to try next.

de Bruijn criterion: only the small kernel re-checks the final proof term. The model and search harness are untrusted — no hallucinated proof survives.

  • Premise selection — retrieve a few of $283{,}067$ Mathlib lemmas (ReProver / LeanDojo).
  • Subgoal decomposition — blueprints & dependency graphs (DeepSeek-Prover-V2, Massot's Blueprint).
  • Expert iteration / RL-from-verifier — the compiler's accept/reject is the reward.
  • Self-correction — feed the Lean error back (Goedel-V2: $88.1\% \to 90.4\%$).

prove p -> (p -> q) -> q

The loop in miniature: hint is the model's "propose", each tactic reshapes the goal, and qed is the kernel's "dispose" — it extracts the λ-term and re-checks its principal type.

Every current system shares this one architecture, so teach the loop once and everything else becomes a refinement of it. The trust story is the de Bruijn criterion: only the small kernel — a couple of thousand lines in Lean — re-checks the final proof term, so the model and the search harness are untrusted and may be arbitrarily buggy without endangering soundness. Walk the four refinements as mechanized versions of what a human prover does anyway: retrieve lemmas, split into subgoals, learn from accept/reject, revise on error. Then send them into the lab's prove command to sit in the proposer's chair for five minutes — hint plays premise selection, undo is the backtracking of best-first search, and qed is the kernel's final re-check.

Case study — EML: the mathematics

One binary operator with the sole constant $1$:

$$ \mathrm{eml}(x,y) \;:=\; \exp(x) - \log(y). $$

$\{\mathrm{eml},1\}$ generates the entire calculator repertoire through a three-production grammar:

$$ T \;::=\; 1 \;\mid\; x_n \;\mid\; \mathrm{eml}(T,T). $$

The analytic analogue of the Sheffer stroke (NAND): a single functionally complete primitive. Formalization: arXiv:2603.21852, Lean 4 + Mathlib $4.28$.

Now the capstone — a real, completed formalization of a research paper, not a classroom toy. One binary operator, exp of x minus log of y, one distinguished constant, three grammar productions — and out of that comes the entire scientific-calculator repertoire: e, pi, arithmetic, every transcendental function. Land the analogy: this is the analytic analogue of the Sheffer stroke, NAND in Boolean logic — a single functionally complete primitive from which everything follows. Every elementary function becomes one fixed-shape binary tree over this single node.

Two witnesses by hand

The simplest constant. $\mathrm{eml}(1,1) = \exp 1 - \log 1 = e - 0 = e$ — a tree of size $K=3$. The EML analogue of $\lnot p = \mathrm{NAND}(p,p)$.

Recovering log from a subtraction (for $z>0$):

$$ \log z \;=\; \mathrm{eml}\bigl(1,\ \mathrm{eml}(\mathrm{eml}(1,z),\,1)\bigr), \qquad K=7. $$

The domain constraint $z>0$ is forced by the innermost $\log z$; outside it the term has no value. Trig follows via Euler: cosTermℂ $= \exp(ix)$, so $\Re = \cos x$ ($K=1273$).

Do both computations live on the board. eml of one and one is e minus zero, so e falls out as a tree of size three — the analogue of "not p equals NAND of p with p", the first thing a single operator gives you for free. Then evaluate the log identity inside-out and let the room watch the exps and logs cancel; point out that the domain constraint z positive is forced by the innermost log, not added by hand. Trigonometry comes via Euler — the cosine witness evaluates to exp of i x and its real part is cosine — and the K-count jumps to 1273, a preview of how witness sizes explode.

Engineering: partial evaluation

Mathlib functions are total (Real.log 0 = 0). Instead of fighting junk values, denote into Option:

noncomputable def EMLTerm.eval? (env : Nat → ℝ) : EMLTerm → Option ℝ
  | .one     => some 1
  | .var n   => some (env n)
  | .eml a b =>
      match EMLTerm.eval? env a, EMLTerm.eval? env b with
      | some va, some vb =>
          if 0 < vb then some (Real.exp va - Real.log vb) else none
      | _, _ => none

A claim is stated only where the witness genuinely has a value — exactly as the paper does.

Here is the engineering idea that made the whole artefact tractable. Mathlib's real functions are total — log of zero is literally zero, a junk value — and instead of fighting that, the denotation is Option-valued and returns none the moment an inner log sees a non-positive argument. Read the guard aloud from the code: "if zero less than vb" is doing all the work. The consequence is honesty by construction — a claim is stated only where the witness genuinely has a value, exactly as the paper does, and bridge theorems never assert equality at a boundary point.

Run it yourself

Feel the "propose" step via functional completeness — rebuild $\lnot,\land,\lor$ from NAND. Open the Lambda Lab and type:

reduce NAND TRUE FALSE

Then reproduce the EML "hello world" in a Lean web editor:

-- e = eml(1,1):  exp 1 − log 1 = e − 0 = e
theorem e_witness : ∃ t : EMLTerm, ∀ env, EMLTerm.eval? env t = some (Real.exp 1) := by
  refine ⟨.eml .one .one, fun env => ?_⟩
  simp [EMLTerm.eval?, Real.log_one]

What "sealed" actually guarantees

  • #print axioms — EML shows only Classical.choice, propext, Quot.sound. "Sorry-free" is not "assumption-free".
  • Seal vs conditional — corollaries gated on EDLTranscendenceBarrier (no instance) are sound but possibly vacuous. Ask if the hypotheses are ever satisfiable.
  • Witness vs completeness — $\exists\,t,\ t.\mathtt{eval?}=v$ proves representability, not completeness, uniqueness or minimality.

Every EML seal has one shape: one literal tree + a kernel-checked equality, under the standard axioms — no more, no less.

The human's job moves upstream: are these the right statements? Is the result interesting? The kernel never decides that.

Close the loop: the skill is auditing a formalization, not trusting a checkmark. #print axioms on EML shows only the three Lean/Mathlib defaults, so "sorry-free" means "assumes classical logic and nothing project-specific" — it is not "assumption-free". The conditional corollaries gated on an unprovided typeclass instance are logically sound but possibly vacuous, and honestly labelled as such — always ask whether the hypotheses are ever satisfiable. And an existential witness proves representability only — not completeness, not uniqueness, not minimality — so the human's job moves upstream: are these the right statements, and is the result interesting? The kernel never decides that.
